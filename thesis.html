T7-like phages infecting native rhizobia

Isolation, host range, and genomic insights into bacteriophages from non-agricultural soils in Western Canada, with potential applications in selective rhizobial biocontrol.

Research Question

Can phages selectively reduce native rhizobia that compete with inoculant strains?

Native rhizobia in soil can compete with introduced inoculant strains for legume nodulation. If the native strains are less effective at nitrogen fixation, this competition can reduce the benefit of inoculation. The thesis investigated whether phages from the same soil environments could selectively infect native rhizobia while sparing useful laboratory or inoculant strains.

1

Competition Problem

Native rhizobia may outcompete inoculant strains in the rhizosphere.

2

Phage Strategy

Find phages that infect native rhizobia with selective host range.

3

Potential Application

Use strictly lytic phages as biocontrol candidates in future soil studies.

Experimental Design

From field samples to phage genome interpretation.

Sample

Collect soil and nodules

Soil and root nodule samples were collected around wild legumes in non-agricultural environments.

Culture

Isolate native rhizobia

Rhizobial strains were isolated from nodules and cultivated for downstream identification and host testing.

Identify

Sequence marker genes

16S rRNA, recA, and atpD sequencing supported taxonomic identification and strain-level comparison.

Trap

Recover phages

Phages were isolated from soil extracts using native isolates and lab strains as trapping hosts.

Test

Measure host range

Double-layer agar assays evaluated which native and laboratory strains each phage could infect.

Analyze

Characterize genomes

TEM, restriction digestion, Nanopore sequencing, RAST, BLAST, and SnapGene supported phage classification.

Findings

Key results from the thesis.

Native rhizobia were identified

Isolates from clovers, vetches, and beans were confirmed within the Rhizobium leguminosarum species complex using marker-gene sequencing and phylogenetic comparison.

21 phages were selected for analysis

Phages were purified from soil samples and selected for further work based on plaque morphology and host interactions.

The phages were T7-like

TEM morphology, genomic features, and sequencing supported classification as T7-like Autographiviridae phages.

Lytic and temperate lifestyles were observed

Most phages behaved as strictly lytic, while a smaller group showed temperate features, including evidence of integration into host genomes.

Host range was selective

Several lytic phages infected multiple native strains while sparing several laboratory strains, supporting their possible value as selective biocontrol candidates.

Future validation is needed

Soil-based and in vivo studies would be needed to evaluate ecological safety and effectiveness outside the laboratory.

Why It Matters

A practical bridge between microbiology, agriculture, and microbial ecology.

The work connects bench microbiology with a real agricultural problem: improving the performance of beneficial inoculant strains by understanding the native microbial competitors and the phages that infect them. It also demonstrates Termeh's experience across sample handling, bacterial culturing, PCR, DNA extraction, host range assays, microscopy preparation, sequencing workflows, bioinformatics-supported interpretation, and scientific communication.
